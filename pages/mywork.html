Beyond Exorcism: Novel Understandings of Mental Illness Through Catholic Art

  • Awarded the Amici di Via Gabina Fellowship to conduct an independent project on portrayals of mental illness in Catholic art in Rome. Awarded Best Research Findings at the Rice University School of Social Sciences Undergraduate Research Symposium.
  • Investigated how the behaviors of Catholic saints, many of which meet modern diagnostic criteria, were venerated rather than pathologized in a religious context.
  • Designed and executed the project independently, drafting grant proposals, managing budget, and completing field research.
    • Collaborated with Dr. Renée Rosfort, Director of the University of Copenhagen Kierkegaard Institute, to formalize and refine findings.
  • Argued that mental illness reflects both objective symptoms/behaviors and subjective social categorization with religious framing transforming “illness” into authority and piety.

Takeaways:

  • Deepened my passion for designing research projects from scratch and drawing connections across disciplines.
  • Showed me the power of societal norms in shaping diagnoses, revealing how psychiatry can reinforce or challenge power structures.
  • AI Connection: As we build artificial minds, we must ensure they don’t simply replicate social biases or enforce norms that marginalize vulnerable groups. Guiding AI’s emerging “theory of mind” responsibly requires an even greater rigor than we apply when questioning our own assumptions.
